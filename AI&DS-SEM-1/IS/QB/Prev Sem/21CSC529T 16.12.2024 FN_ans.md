#### **MEASURES OF DISPERSION - THEORY**

**Definition:** Measures of dispersion quantify the **spread, variability, or scatter** of data points around the central value. While central tendency measures tell us "where" the data is centered, dispersion measures tell us "how spread out" it is.

---

**Key Measures:**

**1. Range**
**Formula:** Range = Maximum - Minimum
- Simplest measure
- Only uses two values
- Sensitive to outliers

**2. Quartile Deviation (Semi-Interquartile Range)**
**Formula:** QD = (Q₃ - Q₁) / 2
- Based on middle 50% of data
- Resistant to outliers

**3. Mean Deviation**
**Formula:** MD = Σ|x - x̄| / n
- Average of absolute deviations from mean

**4. Variance (σ²)**
**Formula:** σ² = Σ(x - μ)² / N (population)
- Average of squared deviations
- Unit: squared original units

**5. Standard Deviation (σ)**
**Formula:** σ = √σ²
- Square root of variance
- Same unit as original data
- Most widely used

**6. Coefficient of Variation (CV)**
**Formula:** CV = (σ / μ) × 100%
- Relative measure (percentage)
- Allows comparison across different datasets

#### **DEFINITIONS**

**COVARIANCE:**
Covariance measures the **joint variability** of two variables—how they change together.

**Formula:**
```
Cov(X,Y) = Σ[(Xi - X̄)(Yi - Ȳ)] / N  (population)
Cov(X,Y) = Σ[(Xi - X̄)(Yi - Ȳ)] / (n-1)  (sample)
```

**Properties:**
- Range: -∞ to +∞
- Units: Product of variable units
- Sign indicates direction of relationship

---

**CORRELATION:**
Correlation is the **standardized measure** of linear relationship between two variables.

**Formula (Pearson's r):**
```
r = Cov(X,Y) / (σx × σy)
```

Or:
```
r = Σ[(Xi - X̄)(Yi - Ȳ)] / √[Σ(Xi - X̄)² × Σ(Yi - Ȳ)²]
```

**Properties:**
- Range: -1 to +1
- Dimensionless (no units)
- Measures both strength and direction

---

```

---

#### **DIFFERENCES BETWEEN COVARIANCE AND CORRELATION**

| Aspect | Covariance | Correlation |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Joint variability | Standardized relationship |
| **Range** | -∞ to +∞ | -1 to +1 |
| **Units** | Product of variable units | Dimensionless |
| **Interpretation** | Direction only | Direction + Strength |
| **Comparability** | Cannot compare across datasets | Can compare across datasets |
| **Scale Dependency** | Depends on scale | Independent of scale |
| **Formula** | Cov(X,Y) = Σ[(X-X̄)(Y-Ȳ)]/N | r = Cov(X,Y)/(σx×σy) |
| **Example Value** | 11,828.4 (hard to interpret) | 0.991 (strong relationship) |

---

#### **SIGNIFICANCE IN UNDERSTANDING RELATIONSHIPS**

**1. COVARIANCE Significance:**

**Advantages:**
- **Mathematical foundation** for correlation
- Used in **portfolio theory** (finance)
- **Covariance matrices** in multivariate analysis
- Essential for **linear regression**

**Limitations:**
- **Hard to interpret magnitude** (is 11,828 large or small?)
- **Scale dependent** (changing units changes covariance)
- Cannot compare relationships across different variable pairs

**Applications:**
- Portfolio diversification (stock covariances)
- Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
- Kalman filtering
- Multivariate normal distributions

---

**2. CORRELATION Significance:**

**Advantages:**
- **Easy to interpret** (-1 to +1 scale)
- **Compare relationships** across different studies
- **Standardized** (unit-free)
- Widely understood measure

**Correlation Strength Guidelines:**
| |r| | Interpretation |
|------|----------------|
| 0.9-1.0 | Very strong |
| 0.7-0.9 | Strong |
| 0.4-0.7 | Moderate |
| 0.1-0.4 | Weak |
| 0.0-0.1 | Very weak/None |

**Applications:**
- **Predictive Modeling:** Feature selection
- **Psychology:** Relationship between variables
- **Medicine:** Risk factor identification
- **Finance:** Asset correlation for diversification
- **Quality Control:** Process variable relationships

---

**3. COMBINED SIGNIFICANCE:**

**In Our Example:**
- **Covariance = 11,828.4:** Tells us direction (positive)
- **Correlation = 0.991:** Tells us strength (very strong)

**Practical Implications:**
- Students who start strong tend to end strong
- **r = 0.991** means beginning score explains 98.2% (r²) of ending score variation
- Very predictable relationship
- Suggests consistent performance or lack of improvement
- May indicate teaching effectiveness or fixed ability

---

**4. DECISION-MAKING APPLICATIONS:**

**Educational Context (our data):**
- High correlation suggests **placements based on beginning scores** would be effective
- May indicate need for **intervention programs** for low starters
- Questions whether instruction made difference

**Research Context:**
- Helps identify **multicollinearity** in regression
- Guides **variable selection** in modeling
- Determines **sample size** requirements

**Business Context:**
- **Sales vs Advertising:** High correlation justifies ad spending
- **Temperature vs Ice Cream Sales:** Plan inventory based on weather
- **Customer satisfaction vs Retention:** Focus on satisfaction initiatives

---

**5. IMPORTANT CAUTIONS:**

**Correlation does NOT imply causation:**
- High r doesn't mean X causes Y
- Could be:
  - Y causes X
  - Third variable causes both
  - Spurious correlation

**Example:** Ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths
- Not causal—both caused by summer weather

**Non-linear relationships:**
- Correlation measures **linear** relationships only
- Can miss curvilinear patterns
- Always visualize with scatter plots

---

**CONCLUSION:**

**Covariance** and **correlation** are complementary:
- **Covariance:** Mathematical tool, foundation for advanced methods
- **Correlation:** Interpretive tool, communicates relationship strength

Both are essential for:
- Understanding **multivariate data**
- **Predictive modeling**
- **Risk assessment**
- **Scientific research**
- **Data-driven decision making**

In practice, **report both**:
- Covariance for technical/mathematical work
- Correlation for interpretation and communication

Our analysis shows students' scores are **highly consistent** (r=0.991), suggesting strong underlying ability factors or stable environmental conditions.

## **NORMAL DISTRIBUTION (GAUSSIAN DISTRIBUTION)**

### **Definition:**
The normal distribution is a **continuous, symmetric, bell-shaped** probability distribution that describes many natural phenomena. It's the most important distribution in statistics.

---

### **Mathematical Formula**

**Probability Density Function:**
```
f(x) = (1 / (σ√(2π))) × e^(-(x-μ)²/(2σ²))
```

Where:
- μ = mean (center of distribution)
- σ = standard deviation (spread)
- π ≈ 3.14159
- e ≈ 2.71828

**Notation:** X ~ N(μ, σ²)

---

### **EXAMPLE: CLASS GRADE DISTRIBUTION**

**Scenario:** Statistics class final exam scores

**Given Data:** Assume exam scores follow N(μ = 75, σ = 10)

| Grade Range | Midpoint | Frequency | Percentage |
|-------------|----------|-----------|------------|
| 50-60 | 55 | 2 | 2.3% |
| 60-70 | 65 | 13 | 14.8% |
| 70-80 | 75 | 50 | 56.8% |
| 80-90 | 85 | 20 | 22.7% |
| 90-100 | 95 | 3 | 3.4% |
| **Total** | | **88** | **100%** |

**Visual Representation:**
```
Frequency
   50 |        █████████
   40 |        █████████
   30 |      ███████████
   20 |    █████████████
   10 |  ███████████████
    0 |_█████████████████__
      50  60  70  80  90 100  Score
             ↑ μ=75
```

---

### **KEY PROPERTIES**

**1. SYMMETRY:**
- Perfectly symmetric about the mean
- Mean = Median = Mode = μ
- Right half mirrors left half

**In our example:**
```
Mean = Median = Mode = 75
Equal number of students above and below 75
```

**2. BELL SHAPE:**
- Single peak at the mean
- Tails extend infinitely in both directions
- Asymptotic (never touches x-axis)

**3. EMPIRICAL RULE (68-95-99.7 Rule):**

```
μ ± 1σ contains 68% of data
μ ± 2σ contains 95% of data
μ ± 3σ contains 99.7% of data
```

**For our exam scores (μ=75, σ=10):**
```
65-85 (75±10): Contains 68% of students (≈60 students)
55-95 (75±20): Contains 95% of students (≈84 students)
45-105 (75±30): Contains 99.7% of students (≈88 students)
```

**Verification from data:**
- 65-85 range: 13+50+20 = 83 students = 94% (close to expected)

**4. TOTAL AREA = 1:**
- Represents 100% probability
- Area under curve between two points = probability of value falling in that range

**5. PARAMETERS:**
- Completely determined by μ and σ
- μ shifts distribution left/right
- σ controls spread (wider/narrower)

**6. INFLECTION POINTS:**
- Occur at μ ± σ
- Where curve changes from convex to concave
- In our example: at 65 and 85

---

### **STANDARD NORMAL DISTRIBUTION (Z-Distribution)**

**Definition:** Normal distribution with μ = 0, σ = 1

**Z-Score Transformation:**
```
Z = (X - μ) / σ
```

**Purpose:**
- Standardizes any normal distribution
- Allows use of standard normal tables
- Enables comparison across different scales

**Example from our data:**
```
Student scored 90:
Z = (90 - 75) / 10 = 1.5

Interpretation: Score is 1.5 standard deviations above mean
```

---

### **CALCULATING PROBABILITIES**

**Example 1:** What proportion of students scored above 85?

**Step 1:** Calculate Z-score
```
Z = (85 - 75) / 10 = 1.0
```

**Step 2:** Look up in Z-table
```
P(Z ≤ 1.0) = 0.8413
P(Z > 1.0) = 1 - 0.8413 = 0.1587
```

**Answer:** 15.87% of students scored above 85 (≈14 students)

---

**Example 2:** What proportion scored between 70 and 80?

**Step 1:** Calculate Z-scores
```
Z₁ = (70 - 75) / 10 = -0.5
Z₂ = (80 - 75) / 10 = 0.5
```

**Step 2:** Find probabilities
```
P(Z ≤ 0.5) = 0.6915
P(Z ≤ -0.5) = 0.3085
P(-0.5 < Z < 0.5) = 0.6915 - 0.3085 = 0.383
```

**Answer:** 38.3% scored between 70-80 (≈34 students)

---

**Example 3:** What score is at the 90th percentile?

**Step 1:** Find Z-score for 90th percentile
```
From Z-table: Z = 1.28
```

**Step 2:** Convert to X
```
X = μ + Zσ
X = 75 + 1.28(10)
X = 75 + 12.8
X = 87.8
```

**Answer:** Score of 88 is at 90th percentile (A grade threshold)

---

### **SIGNIFICANCE IN STATISTICS**

**1. CENTRAL LIMIT THEOREM (CLT):**

**Most Important Result in Statistics:**
```
Sample means approach normal distribution
as sample size increases, regardless of
population distribution
```

**Implications:**
- Makes inference possible even when population isn't normal
- Justifies using Z-tests and t-tests
- Foundation of statistical inference

**Example:**
Even if individual quiz scores aren't normal, the average of 30 quizzes **will be** approximately normal.

---

**2. BASIS FOR STATISTICAL TESTS:**

**Tests assuming normality:**
- Z-test (large samples)
- t-test (small samples)
- ANOVA
- Linear regression
- Many others

---

**3. CONFIDENCE INTERVALS:**

**For our exam scores:**
```
95% CI for mean = X̄ ± 1.96(σ/√n)
```

If we sample 25 students:
```
SE = 10/√25 = 2
95% CI = 75 ± 1.96(2) = (71.08, 78.92)
```

---

**4. HYPOTHESIS TESTING:**

Test if teaching method improved scores from μ₀=70 to μ=75:
```
H₀: μ = 70
H₁: μ > 70
Z = (75 - 70) / (10/√88) = 5/1.07 = 4.67
P-value < 0.001 (highly significant)
```

---

**5. QUALITY CONTROL:**

**Six Sigma:** μ ± 6σ captures 99.9999998% of production

For exam scores:
```
±3σ control limits: (45, 105)
Scores outside this range are unusual
```

### **ADVANTAGES OF NORMAL DISTRIBUTION**

**1. Mathematical Properties:**

### Part (ii) - Car Manufacturer Bayes' Problem

**Question:** A car manufacturer produces three models: Sedan, SUV, and Coupe. The probability of a defect is 0.02 for Sedan, 0.05 for SUV, and 0.03 for Coupe. Given a defective car, the probability it is a Sedan is 0.3, an SUV is 0.4, and a Coupe is 0.3. If a defective car is selected, which model is it most likely to be? Explain with calculations.

---

**Given Information:**

**Defect Rates (Prior Information):**
- P(Defective | Sedan) = 0.02
- P(Defective | SUV) = 0.05
- P(Defective | Coupe) = 0.03

**Posterior Probabilities (Given):**
- P(Sedan | Defective) = 0.3
- P(SUV | Defective) = 0.4
- P(Coupe | Defective) = 0.3

---

#### **DIRECT ANSWER**

Since the **posterior probabilities** are already provided:

```
P(Sedan | Defective) = 0.3 = 30%
P(SUV | Defective) = 0.4 = 40%
P(Coupe | Defective) = 0.3 = 30%
```

**Comparison:**
- SUV: 40% (highest)
- Sedan: 30%
- Coupe: 30%

**ANSWER: SUV is the most likely model** (40% probability)

---

#### **UNDERSTANDING THROUGH BAYES' THEOREM**

Let's verify and understand **why** this makes sense.

**Bayes' Theorem:**
```
P(Model | Defective) = [P(Defective | Model) × P(Model)] / P(Defective)
```

---

**Step 1: Work backwards to find production proportions**

Let:
- P(Sedan) = pS
- P(SUV) = pV
- P(Coupe) = pC

**From Bayes' theorem:**
```
P(Sedan | Defective) = [0.02 × pS] / P(Defective)
0.3 = [0.02 × pS] / P(Defective)

P(SUV | Defective) = [0.05 × pV] / P(Defective)
0.4 = [0.05 × pV] / P(Defective)

P(Coupe | Defective) = [0.03 × pC] / P(Defective)
0.3 = [0.03 × pC] / P(Defective)
```

---

**Step 2: Find relative production rates**

**Divide first by second equation:**
```
0.3/0.4 = (0.02 × pS)/(0.05 × pV)
0.75 = 0.02pS / 0.05pV
0.75 × 0.05pV = 0.02pS
0.0375pV = 0.02pS
pS = 1.875pV
```

**Divide second by third equation:**
```
0.4/0.3 = (0.05 × pV)/(0.03 × pC)
1.333 = 0.05pV / 0.03pC
1.333 × 0.03pC = 0.05pV
0.04pC = 0.05pV
pV = 0.8pC
pC = 1.25pV
```

---

**Step 3: Express all in terms of pV**

Let pV = 1 unit (base):
```
pS = 1.875 units
pV = 1 unit
pC = 1.25 units
Total = 4.125 units
```

**Normalized probabilities:**
```
P(Sedan) = 1.875 / 4.125 = 0.455 or 45.5%
P(SUV) = 1 / 4.125 = 0.242 or 24.2%
P(Coupe) = 1.25 / 4.125 = 0.303 or 30.3%
```

---

**Step 4: Calculate overall defect rate**

```
P(Defective) = P(D|Sedan)×P(Sedan) + P(D|SUV)×P(SUV) + P(D|Coupe)×P(Coupe)

P(Defective) = 0.02(0.455) + 0.05(0.242) + 0.03(0.303)
P(Defective) = 0.0091 + 0.0121 + 0.0091
P(Defective) = 0.0303 or 3.03%
```

---

**Step 5: Verify using Bayes' Theorem**

**For Sedan:**
```
P(Sedan | Defective) = (0.02 × 0.455) / 0.0303
                      = 0.0091 / 0.0303
                      = 0.300 or 30% ✓
```

**For SUV:**
```
P(SUV | Defective) = (0.05 × 0.242) / 0.0303
                    = 0.0121 / 0.0303
                    = 0.399 ≈ 40% ✓
```

**For Coupe:**
```
P(Coupe | Defective) = (0.03 × 0.303) / 0.0303
                      = 0.0091 / 0.0303
                      = 0.300 or 30% ✓
```

#### **DETAILED ANALYSIS**

  - Net: 30% of defects

### Part (i) - Rules for Computing Probability

**Question:** Explain the rules for computing probability, including the addition and multiplication rules, with appropriate examples.

---

#### **BASIC PROBABILITY CONCEPTS**

**Definition:** Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring.

**Formula:**
```
P(Event) = (Number of favorable outcomes) / (Total number of possible outcomes)
```

**Properties:**
- 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1
- P(Certain event) = 1
- P(Impossible event) = 0
- P(A') = 1 - P(A) (complement rule)

---

#### **RULE 1: ADDITION RULE**

**For Mutually Exclusive Events (OR):**

Events that **cannot occur simultaneously**.

**Formula:**
```
P(A or B) = P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)
```

**Example 1:** Rolling a die
```
P(Rolling 2 or 5) = P(2) + P(5) = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3
```

**Explanation:** You cannot roll both 2 and 5 simultaneously, so events are mutually exclusive.

---

**For Non-Mutually Exclusive Events:**

Events that **can occur simultaneously**.

**Formula:**
```
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)
```

**Why subtract P(A and B)?** To avoid double-counting the overlap.

**Example 2:** Drawing from deck of cards
```
P(Heart or King) = ?

P(Heart) = 13/52
P(King) = 4/52
P(King of Hearts) = 1/52

P(Heart or King) = 13/52 + 4/52 - 1/52 = 16/52 = 4/13
```

**Visualization:**
```
Hearts: 13 cards (including King of Hearts)
Kings: 4 cards (including King of Hearts)
Overlap: 1 card (King of Hearts)
Total unique: 13 + 4 - 1 = 16
```

---

**Example 3:** Student performance
```
In a class:
- 60% students passed Math (M)
- 50% students passed Physics (P)
- 30% students passed both

P(Passed Math or Physics) = ?

P(M or P) = 0.60 + 0.50 - 0.30 = 0.80 or 80%
```

**Interpretation:** 80% passed at least one subject.

---

#### **RULE 2: MULTIPLICATION RULE**

**For Independent Events (AND):**

Events where occurrence of one **doesn't affect** the other.

**Formula:**
```
P(A and B) = P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
```

**Example 4:** Coin toss and die roll
```
P(Heads and Rolling 6) = P(H) × P(6)
                        = 1/2 × 1/6
                        = 1/12
```

**Explanation:** Coin result doesn't affect die outcome—independent events.

---

**Example 5:** Quality control
```
Two machines work independently:
- Machine A works properly: 95%
- Machine B works properly: 90%

P(Both work) = 0.95 × 0.90 = 0.855 or 85.5%
```

---

**For Dependent Events (Conditional):**

Events where occurrence of one **affects** the other.

**Formula:**
```
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
```

Where P(B|A) = Probability of B given A has occurred

**Example 6:** Drawing cards without replacement
```
P(Two Aces in a row) = ?

P(First Ace) = 4/52
P(Second Ace | First Ace) = 3/51  (only 3 aces left in 51 cards)

P(Two Aces) = (4/52) × (3/51)
            = 12/2652
            = 1/221
            ≈ 0.0045 or 0.45%
```

---

**Example 7:** Medical testing
```
Disease prevalence: 1%
Test sensitivity: 95% (correctly detects disease)
P(Have disease and test positive) = ?

P(Disease and Positive) = P(Disease) × P(Positive|Disease)
                        = 0.01 × 0.95
                        = 0.0095 or 0.95%
```

---

#### **RULE 3: CONDITIONAL PROBABILITY**

**Formula:**
```
P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B)
```

**Example 8:** Student grades
```
Given data:
- 40% students study regularly (S)
- 80% of regular studiers pass (P)
- 30% of non-studiers pass

P(Pass | Study) = 0.80 (given)

P(Study and Pass) = P(S) × P(P|S) = 0.40 × 0.80 = 0.32 or 32%
```

---

#### **RULE 4: TOTAL PROBABILITY THEOREM**

When sample space is partitioned into mutually exclusive events B₁, B₂, ..., Bₙ:

**Formula:**
```
P(A) = P(A|B₁)P(B₁) + P(A|B₂)P(B₂) + ... + P(A|Bₙ)P(Bₙ)
```

**Example 9:** Manufacturing from three plants
```
Plant A produces 50% of items (defect rate 2%)
Plant B produces 30% of items (defect rate 4%)
Plant C produces 20% of items (defect rate 5%)

P(Defective) = ?

P(D) = P(D|A)P(A) + P(D|B)P(B) + P(D|C)P(C)
     = 0.02(0.50) + 0.04(0.30) + 0.05(0.20)
     = 0.010 + 0.012 + 0.010
     = 0.032 or 3.2%
```

---

#### **RULE 5: BAYES' THEOREM**

**Formula:**
```
P(B|A) = [P(A|B) × P(B)] / P(A)
```

**Example 10:** Email spam filtering
```
Given:
- 70% of emails are spam (S)
- 5% of spam contains word "winner" (W)
- 0.1% of legitimate emails contain "winner"

P(Spam | Contains "winner") = ?

Step 1: Find P(Winner)
P(W) = P(W|S)P(S) + P(W|S')P(S')
     = 0.05(0.70) + 0.001(0.30)
     = 0.035 + 0.0003
     = 0.0353

Step 2: Apply Bayes' Theorem
P(S|W) = [P(W|S) × P(S)] / P(W)
       = [0.05 × 0.70] / 0.0353
       = 0.035 / 0.0353
       = 0.992 or 99.2%
```

**Interpretation:** Email with "winner" is 99.2% likely to be spam.

---

#### **COMPREHENSIVE EXAMPLE: MEDICAL DIAGNOSIS**

**Problem:** Disease affects 0.5% of population. Test has 99% sensitivity and 95% specificity. Patient tests positive. What's probability they have disease?

**Given:**
- P(Disease) = 0.005
- P(Positive | Disease) = 0.99 (sensitivity)
- P(Negative | No Disease) = 0.95, so P(Positive | No Disease) = 0.05

**Find:** P(Disease | Positive)

**Step 1: Calculate P(Positive) using Total Probability**
```
P(Pos) = P(Pos|D)P(D) + P(Pos|D')P(D')
       = 0.99(0.005) + 0.05(0.995)
       = 0.00495 + 0.04975
       = 0.0547
```

**Step 2: Apply Bayes' Theorem**
```
P(D|Pos) = [P(Pos|D) × P(D)] / P(Pos)
         = [0.99 × 0.005] / 0.0547
         = 0.00495 / 0.0547
         = 0.0905 or 9.05%
```

**Surprising Result:** Despite 99% accurate test, only 9% chance of having disease!

**Why?** Base rate (0.5%) is very low—most positives are false positives.

#### **SUMMARY OF PROBABILITY RULES**

| Rule | Formula | Use Case |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Addition (Exclusive)** | P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) | Events can't occur together |
| **Addition (Inclusive)** | P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A∩B) | Events can overlap |
| **Multiplication (Independent)** | P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B) | Independent events |
| **Multiplication (Dependent)** | P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B\|A) | Dependent events |
| **Complement** | P(A') = 1 - P(A) | Opposite event |
| **Conditional** | P(A\|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B) | Given information |
| **Total Probability** | P(A) = ΣP(A\|Bᵢ)P(Bᵢ) | Partitioned sample space |
| **Bayes' Theorem** | P(B\|A) = P(A\|B)P(B) / P(A) | Reverse conditional |

### Part (ii) - Permutation vs Combination

**Question:** What is the fundamental difference between permutation and combination with an example?

---

#### **DEFINITIONS**

**PERMUTATION:**
Arrangement of objects where **order matters**.

**Formula:**
```
P(n,r) = nPr = n! / (n-r)!
```

Where:
- n = total objects
- r = objects selected
- n! = n × (n-1) × (n-2) × ... × 1

---

**COMBINATION:**
Selection of objects where **order doesn't matter**.

**Formula:**
```
C(n,r) = nCr = n! / [r! × (n-r)!]
```

---

#### **KEY DIFFERENCE**

| Aspect | Permutation | Combination |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Order** | Matters | Doesn't matter |
| **Focus** | Arrangement | Selection |
| **Symbol** | ⁿPᵣ or P(n,r) | ⁿCᵣ or C(n,r) |
| **Formula** | n!/(n-r)! | n!/[r!(n-r)!] |
| **Result** | Larger | Smaller |
| **Example** | Password | Committee |

**Relationship:**
```
nPr = nCr × r!
```

Permutations = Combinations × (arrangements of selected items)

---

#### **EXAMPLE 1: SIMPLE ILLUSTRATION**

**Problem:** Select 2 letters from {A, B, C}

**Permutations (Order Matters):**
```
AB ≠ BA (different arrangements)

All permutations:
AB, BA, AC, CA, BC, CB

Using formula:
³P₂ = 3! / (3-2)! = 3! / 1! = 6 / 1 = 6 ✓
```

**Combinations (Order Doesn't Matter):**
```
AB = BA (same selection)

All combinations:
AB, AC, BC (only 3 unique selections)

Using formula:
³C₂ = 3! / (2! × 1!) = 6 / (2 × 1) = 3 ✓
```

**Verification:**
```
³P₂ = ³C₂ × 2!
6 = 3 × 2 ✓
```

---

#### **EXAMPLE 2: RACE MEDALS**

**Problem:** 10 runners compete. How many ways to award Gold, Silver, Bronze?

**This is PERMUTATION** (order matters—Gold ≠ Silver ≠ Bronze)

**Formula:**
```
¹⁰P₃ = 10! / (10-3)!
     = 10! / 7!
     = 10 × 9 × 8
     = 720
```

**Answer: 720 ways**

**Why not combination?**
- Selecting runners {A, B, C} can give:
  - A-Gold, B-Silver, C-Bronze
  - A-Gold, C-Silver, B-Bronze
  - B-Gold, A-Silver, C-Bronze
  - ... (6 different outcomes!)
- **Position/rank matters**

---

#### **EXAMPLE 3: COMMITTEE FORMATION**

**Problem:** Select 3 members from 10 people for a committee.

**This is COMBINATION** (order doesn't matter—all members equal)

**Formula:**
```
¹⁰C₃ = 10! / (3! × 7!)
     = (10 × 9 × 8) / (3 × 2 × 1)
     = 720 / 6
     = 120
```

**Answer: 120 ways**

**Why not permutation?**
- Selecting {John, Mary, Bob} is **same committee** as {Mary, Bob, John}
- **No hierarchy/order**

---

#### **EXAMPLE 4: PASSWORD CREATION**

**Problem:** Create 4-digit password from digits {1, 2, 3, 4, 5} without repetition.

**This is PERMUTATION** (order matters—1234 ≠ 4321)

**Formula:**
```
⁵P₄ = 5! / (5-4)!
    = 5! / 1!
    = 120 / 1
    = 120
```

**Answer: 120 different passwords**

**Step-by-step:**
```
Position 1: 5 choices
Position 2: 4 choices (one used)
Position 3: 3 choices
Position 4: 2 choices

Total: 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 = 120 ✓
```

---

#### **EXAMPLE 5: PIZZA TOPPINGS**

**Problem:** Choose 3 toppings from {Pepperoni, Mushroom, Olives, Onion, Peppers}.

**This is COMBINATION** (order doesn't matter)

**Formula:**
```
⁵C₃ = 5! / (3! × 2!)
    = (5 × 4 × 3!) / (3! × 2 × 1)
    = (5 × 4) / 2
    = 20 / 2
    = 10
```

**Answer: 10 different pizzas**

**Why?**
- {Pepperoni, Mushroom, Olives} tastes same as {Olives, Pepperoni, Mushroom}
- **Order of adding toppings doesn't matter**

---

#### **EXAMPLE 6: COMPREHENSIVE PROBLEM**

**Scenario:** Student club has 15 members. Need to select:
1. President, VP, and Secretary (distinct roles)
2. 3-person event committee (equal members)

**Part 1: Officers (PERMUTATION)**
```
¹⁵P₃ = 15! / 12!
     = 15 × 14 × 13
     = 2,730 ways
```

**Part 2: Committee (COMBINATION)**
```
¹⁵C₃ = 15! / (3! × 12!)
     = (15 × 14 × 13) / (3 × 2 × 1)
     = 2,730 / 6
     = 455 ways
```

**Comparison:**
- **6 times more** permutations than combinations (factor of 3!)
- Same 3 people, but **roles create more arrangements**

---

#### **WHEN TO USE WHICH?**

**Use PERMUTATION when:**
- ✓ Positions/ranks exist (1st, 2nd, 3rd)
- ✓ Order creates different outcomes
- ✓ Arranging/sequencing items
- ✓ Examples: Race results, passwords, seating arrangements, lock codes

**Use COMBINATION when:**
- ✓ Selecting/choosing items
- ✓ Order doesn't matter
- ✓ Forming groups with equal members
- ✓ Examples: Committees, teams, hands of cards, lottery numbers

---

#### **FORMULA COMPARISON**

**For selecting 3 from 5:**

**Permutation:**
```
⁵P₃ = 5!/(5-3)! = 5×4×3 = 60
```

**Combination:**
```
⁵C₃ = 5!/(3!×2!) = (5×4×3)/(3×2×1) = 60/6 = 10
```

**Ratio:**
```
⁵P₃ / ⁵C₃ = 60/10 = 6 = 3!
```

Every combination has 3! = 6 permutations.

---

#### **SPECIAL CASES**

**1. Selecting All Items:**
```
Permutation: ⁿPₙ = n!
Combination: ⁿCₙ = 1

Example: 5P₅ = 120, but ⁵C₅ = 1
(120 ways to arrange 5 items, but only 1 way to select all)
```

**2. Selecting One Item:**
```
Permutation: ⁿP₁ = n
Combination: ⁿC₁ = n

Example: ⁵P₁ = ⁵C₁ = 5
(Same—no arrangement needed for single item)
```

**3. Selecting None:**
```
ⁿC₀ = 1 (one way to select nothing)
```

---

#### **REAL-WORLD APPLICATION**

**Lottery:**
- Pick 6 numbers from 49
- **Combination** used (order doesn't matter)
```
⁴⁹C₆ = 49!/(6!×43!) = 13,983,816 combinations
```

**If it were permutation:**
```
⁴⁹P₆ = 49!/43! = 10,068,347,520 permutations
```

720 times harder! (6! = 720)

---

**CONCLUSION:**

**Fundamental Difference:**
- **Permutation:** "How many ways can we **arrange**?"
- **Combination:** "How many ways can we **choose**?"

**Memory Aid:**
- **P**ermutation → **P**osition matters
- **C**ombination → **C**hoose without order

The relationship **nPr = nCr × r!** shows that permutations account for all possible arrangements of each combination.

### Part (iii) - Monthly Electricity Bill Problem

**Question:** The monthly electricity bill of households in a city follows a normal distribution, with a mean of $120 and a variance of $49. What is the probability that a randomly selected household has a monthly bill less than $110?

---

**Given:**
- Mean (μ) = $120
- Variance (σ²) = $49
- Find: P(X < $110)

---

**Step 1: Calculate Standard Deviation**

**Formula:** σ = √σ²

```
σ = √49
σ = $7
```

**Standard Deviation = $7**

---

**Step 2: Standardize using Z-score**

**Formula:** Z = (X - μ) / σ

```
Z = (110 - 120) / 7
Z = -10 / 7
Z = -1.43
```

**Z-score = -1.43**

**Interpretation:** $110 is 1.43 standard deviations **below** the mean.

---

**Step 3: Find Probability from Z-table**

**We need:** P(Z < -1.43)

From standard normal table:
```
P(Z < -1.43) = 0.0764
```

Or using symmetry:
```
P(Z < -1.43) = P(Z > 1.43) = 1 - P(Z < 1.43)
P(Z < 1.43) = 0.9236
P(Z < -1.43) = 1 - 0.9236 = 0.0764
```

---

**Step 4: Final Answer**

**P(Bill < $110) = 0.0764 or 7.64%**

---

#### **INTERPRETATION**

**1. Probability:**
- About **7.64%** of households have bills less than $110
- If city has 10,000 households, approximately **764 households** have bills under $110

**2. Percentile:**
- $110 bill is at the **7.64th percentile**
- It's relatively low—most households pay more

**3. Distance from Mean:**
- $110 is $10 below average ($120)
- This represents 1.43 standard deviations below mean

---

#### **VERIFICATION USING EMPIRICAL RULE**

**68-95-99.7 Rule:**
```
μ ± 1σ = 120 ± 7 = ($113, $127) contains 68%
μ ± 2σ = 120 ± 14 = ($106, $134) contains 95%
μ ± 3σ = 120 ± 21 = ($99, $141) contains 99.7%
```

**$110 is between μ - 2σ and μ - 1σ:**
```
Below μ - 1σ ($113): (100% - 68%)/2 = 16%
Below μ - 2σ ($106): (100% - 95%)/2 = 2.5%

$110 is between these, so:
P(X < $110) should be between 2.5% and 16% ✓
7.64% fits perfectly! ✓
```

---

#### **ADDITIONAL CALCULATIONS**

**1. What proportion pay more than $110?**
```
P(X > $110) = 1 - P(X < $110)
            = 1 - 0.0764

## Question 4 (20 Marks)

### Part (i) - Hypothesis Testing Steps

**Question:** Explain the steps involved in hypothesis testing and the importance of formulating null and alternative hypotheses. Provide an example.

---

#### **WHAT IS HYPOTHESIS TESTING?**

**Definition:** Statistical method to make decisions about population parameters based on sample data.

**Purpose:**
- Test claims about populations
- Determine if observed differences are real or due to chance
- Make evidence-based decisions

---

#### **THE 7 STEPS OF HYPOTHESIS TESTING**

---

### **STEP 1: STATE THE HYPOTHESES**

**Null Hypothesis (H₀):**
- Statement of "no effect" or "no difference"
- Assumes status quo
- Always contains equality (=, ≤, ≥)

**Alternative Hypothesis (H₁ or Hₐ):**
- What we want to prove
- Statement of effect or difference
- Contains inequality (<, >, ≠)

**Types of Tests:**

**1. Two-tailed:**
```
H₀: μ = μ₀
H₁: μ ≠ μ₀
```

**2. Right-tailed:**
```
H₀: μ ≤ μ₀
H₁: μ > μ₀
```

**3. Left-tailed:**
```
H₀: μ ≥ μ₀
H₁: μ < μ₀
```

---

### **STEP 2: CHOOSE SIGNIFICANCE LEVEL (α)**

**Definition:** Probability of rejecting H₀ when it's true (Type I error)

**Common values:**
- α = 0.05 (5%) - most common
- α = 0.01 (1%) - more stringent
- α = 0.10 (10%) - more lenient

**Interpretation:**
- α = 0.05 means 5% risk of false positive
- Willing to be wrong 5% of the time

---

### **STEP 3: SELECT TEST STATISTIC**

Choose based on:
- Sample size
- Known/unknown population variance
- Type of data

**Common Test Statistics:**

| Situation | Test | Formula |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Large sample (n≥30), σ known | Z-test | Z = (X̄-μ₀)/(σ/√n) |
| Small sample (n<30), σ unknown | t-test | t = (X̄-μ₀)/(s/√n) |
| Proportions | Z-test | Z = (p̂-p₀)/√[p₀(1-p₀)/n] |
| Variance | χ²-test | χ² = (n-1)s²/σ₀² |

---

### **STEP 4: DETERMINE DECISION RULE**

**Critical Value Approach:**
- Find critical value from table
- Define rejection region

**For two-tailed test (α=0.05):**
```
Reject H₀ if: Z < -1.96 or Z > 1.96
```

**For right-tailed test (α=0.05):**
```
Reject H₀ if: Z > 1.645
```

**For left-tailed test (α=0.05):**
```
Reject H₀ if: Z < -1.645
```

---

### **STEP 5: COLLECT DATA AND CALCULATE TEST STATISTIC**

- Gather sample data
- Calculate sample statistics (X̄, s)
- Compute test statistic value

---

### **STEP 6: MAKE DECISION**

**Two methods:**

**1. Critical Value Method:**
- Compare test statistic to critical value
- If in rejection region → Reject H₀
- If not → Fail to reject H₀

**2. P-value Method:**
- Calculate p-value (probability of observing data if H₀ true)
- If p-value ≤ α → Reject H₀
- If p-value > α → Fail to reject H₀

---

### **STEP 7: STATE CONCLUSION**

**In context of problem:**
- Technical conclusion (about H₀)
- Practical interpretation (what it means)

---

#### **IMPORTANCE OF NULL AND ALTERNATIVE HYPOTHESES**

**1. STRUCTURAL FRAMEWORK:**
- Provides clear decision framework
- Defines what we're testing
- Establishes burden of proof

**2. LOGICAL FOUNDATION:**
- **H₀** assumes innocence (like court: "innocent until proven guilty")
- **H₁** requires strong evidence to accept
- Prevents jumping to conclusions

**3. ERROR CONTROL:**
- **Type I Error (α):** Reject true H₀ (false positive)
- **Type II Error (β):** Fail to reject false H₀ (false negative)
- Hypotheses allow us to quantify and control these errors

**4. OBJECTIVE DECISION-MAKING:**
- Removes subjectivity
- Pre-specified criteria
- Reproducible results

**5. SCIENTIFIC RIGOR:**
- Standard methodology across fields
- Enables peer review
- Builds cumulative knowledge

---

#### **COMPREHENSIVE EXAMPLE: NEW TEACHING METHOD**

**Scenario:** A university wants to test if a new teaching method improves exam scores. Historical mean score is 75. They pilot the new method with 36 students.

---

**STEP 1: State Hypotheses**

**Claim:** New method increases scores

```
H₀: μ ≤ 75 (new method doesn't improve or makes worse)
H₁: μ > 75 (new method improves scores)
```

**Type:** Right-tailed test (we only care if scores increase)

---

**STEP 2: Choose Significance Level**

```
α = 0.05 (5% significance level)
```

**Meaning:** 5% risk of concluding method works when it doesn't.

---

**STEP 3: Select Test Statistic**

**Given:**
- n = 36 (sample size)
- n ≥ 30 (large sample)

**Use Z-test:**
```
Z = (X̄ - μ₀) / (σ/√n)
```

**Assume population σ = 10** (known from historical data)

---

**STEP 4: Determine Decision Rule**

**For right-tailed test at α = 0.05:**

Critical value from Z-table: Z_critical = 1.645

**Decision Rule:**
```
Reject H₀ if Z > 1.645
Fail to reject H₀ if Z ≤ 1.645
```

**Rejection Region:** Z > 1.645

---

**STEP 5: Collect Data and Calculate**

**Sample Results:**
- n = 36 students
- X̄ = 78 (sample mean)
- σ = 10 (population SD)

**Calculate Test Statistic:**
```
Z = (X̄ - μ₀) / (σ/√n)
Z = (78 - 75) / (10/√36)
Z = 3 / (10/6)
Z = 3 / 1.667
Z = 1.80
```

**Test Statistic: Z = 1.80**

---

**STEP 6: Make Decision**

**Method 1: Critical Value Approach**
```
Z = 1.80 > Z_critical = 1.645
Test statistic falls in rejection region
→ Reject H₀
```

**Method 2: P-value Approach**
```
P-value = P(Z > 1.80)
From Z-table: P(Z < 1.80) = 0.9641
P-value = 1 - 0.9641 = 0.0359

P-value (0.0359) < α (0.05)
→ Reject H₀
```

---

**STEP 7: State Conclusion**

**Technical Conclusion:**
"At the 5% significance level, we reject the null hypothesis."

**Practical Interpretation:**
"There is sufficient evidence to conclude that the new teaching method significantly improves exam scores. The average score of 78 with the new method is statistically significantly higher than the historical average of 75."

**Detailed Explanation:**
- Observed improvement: 3 points (78 - 75)
- This improvement is unlikely due to chance (p = 0.0359)
- Only 3.59% probability of seeing this result if method had no effect
- University can adopt new method with confidence

---

#### **UNDERSTANDING P-VALUE**

**P-value = 0.0359 means:**
- If new method had NO effect (H₀ true)
- Probability of observing X̄ ≥ 78 is only 3.59%
- This is rare enough to doubt H₀
- Evidence favors H₁

**Visual Representation:**
```
        Standard Normal Distribution
             |
           __|__
          /  |  \
        /    |    \
      /      |      \
    /________|________\____
            75     78    Z-axis
            ↑       ↑
           μ₀   Observed
                 Z=1.80

    Shaded area right of Z=1.80 = 0.0359 (p-value)
    This falls in rejection region (beyond 1.645)
```

---

#### **TYPES OF ERRORS**

**Type I Error (α):**
- **Definition:** Reject H₀ when it's true
- **In our example:** Conclude method works when it doesn't
- **Probability:** α = 0.05 (5%)
- **Consequence:** Waste resources implementing ineffective method

**Type II Error (β):**
- **Definition:** Fail to reject H₀ when it's false
- **In our example:** Miss discovering an effective method
- **Probability:** Depends on true effect size
- **Consequence:** Miss opportunity for improvement

**Power of Test:**
```
Power = 1 - β
Probability of correctly rejecting false H₀
```

**Trade-off:**
- Decreasing α increases β
- Need balance based on consequences
- Sample size helps: larger n reduces both errors

---

#### **PRACTICAL CONSIDERATIONS**

**1. Statistical vs Practical Significance:**
- **Statistical:** P-value < α (we achieved this)
- **Practical:** Is 3-point improvement worth the cost?
- Large samples can detect tiny effects that may not matter practically

**2. Confidence Interval Alternative:**

**For our example:**

95% CI for μ = X̄ ± Z₀.₀₂₅(σ/√n)
              = 78 ± 1.96(10/6)
              = 78 ± 3.27
              = (74.73, 81.27)
```

**Interpretation:** We're 95% confident true mean is between 74.73 and 81.27.

**Since 75 is barely in this interval, result is marginally significant—consistent with p = 0.0359.**

**3. Assumptions Check:**
- Random sampling ✓
- Independent observations ✓
- Normal distribution or large n (n=36 ≥ 30) ✓
- Known or accurate σ (assumed) ✓

#### **REAL-WORLD APPLICATIONS**

**1. Healthcare:**
```
H₀: New drug has no effect
H₁: New drug reduces symptoms
Critical for FDA approval
```

**2. Manufacturing:**
```
H₀: Product meets specifications
H₁: Product defective
Quality control decisions
```

**3. Marketing:**
```

Investment justification
```

**4. Criminal Justice:**
```
H₀: Defendant is innocent
H₁: Defendant is guilty
Type I error: Convict innocent (serious!)
Type II error: Acquit guilty
```

---

**SUMMARY:**

Hypothesis testing provides:
1. **Structured approach** to decision-making
2. **Quantifiable risk** of errors
3. **Objective criteria** for conclusions
4. **Reproducible** methodology
5. **Balance** between caution and discovery

The null hypothesis serves as the **default position**, requiring substantial evidence to reject—preventing hasty conclusions while allowing discovery of real effects when present.

### Part (ii) - Central Limit Theorem Problem

**Question:** A random sample of size 100 is taken from a population whose mean is 60 and variance 400. Estimate using central limit theorem, with what probability can we assert that mean of the sample will not differ from μ = 60 by more than 4.

---

**Given:**
- n = 100 (sample size)
- μ = 60 (population mean)
- σ² = 400 (population variance)
- Find: P(|X̄ - 60| ≤ 4)

---

**Step 1: Calculate Population Standard Deviation**

**Formula:** σ = √σ²

```
σ = √400
σ = 20
```

**Population Standard Deviation: σ = 20**

---

**Step 2: Apply Central Limit Theorem**

**Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states:**
For large sample sizes (n ≥ 30), the sampling distribution of X̄ is approximately normal:

```
X̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n)
```

**For our problem:**
```
X̄ ~ N(60, 400/100)
X̄ ~ N(60, 4)
```

**Standard Error:**
```
SE = σ/√n = 20/√100 = 20/10 = 2
```

**So: X̄ ~ N(60, 2²)**

---

**Step 3: Rewrite the Probability Statement**

**We want:** P(|X̄ - 60| ≤ 4)

This means:
```
P(-4 ≤ X̄ - 60 ≤ 4)
= P(60 - 4 ≤ X̄ ≤ 60 + 4)
= P(56 ≤ X̄ ≤ 64)
```

---

**Step 4: Standardize to Z-scores**

**Formula:** Z = (X̄ - μ) / SE

**For X̄ = 56:**
```
Z₁ = (56 - 60) / 2
Z₁ = -4 / 2
Z₁ = -2.0
```

**For X̄ = 64:**
```
Z₂ = (64 - 60) / 2
Z₂ = 4 / 2
Z₂ = 2.0
```

---

**Step 5: Find Probability from Z-table**

**We need:** P(-2.0 ≤ Z ≤ 2.0)

From standard normal table:
```
P(Z ≤ 2.0) = 0.9772
P(Z ≤ -2.0) = 0.0228
```

**Calculate:**
```
P(-2.0 ≤ Z ≤ 2.0) = P(Z ≤ 2.0) - P(Z ≤ -2.0)
                    = 0.9772 - 0.0228
                    = 0.9544
```

---

**FINAL ANSWER:**

**P(|X̄ - 60| ≤ 4) = 0.9544 or 95.44%**

---

#### **INTERPRETATION**

**1. Main Result:**
- We can assert with **95.44% confidence** that the sample mean will not differ from the population mean by more than 4
- In other words, there's about **95.44% probability** that X̄ will fall between 56 and 64

**2. Practical Meaning:**
- If we repeatedly take samples of size 100
- About 95.44% of the time, sample mean will be within ±4 of 60
- Only 4.56% of the time will it differ by more than 4

**3. Margin of Error:**
- ±4 represents the margin of error
- With 95.44% confidence
- This corresponds to approximately ±2 standard errors (2 × SE = 2 × 2 = 4)

---

#### **VERIFICATION USING EMPIRICAL RULE**

**For normal distribution:**
- μ ± 1σ contains ~68% of data
- μ ± 2σ contains ~95% of data
- μ ± 3σ contains ~99.7% of data

**For our sampling distribution:**
```
SE = 2

μ ± 1(SE) = 60 ± 2 = (58, 62) → ~68%
μ ± 2(SE) = 60 ± 4 = (56, 64) → ~95% ✓
μ ± 3(SE) = 60 ± 6 = (54, 66) → ~99.7%
```

Our answer of 95.44% matches the empirical rule for ±2σ! ✓

---

#### **WHY CENTRAL LIMIT THEOREM MATTERS HERE**

**Key Points:**

**1. Unknown Population Distribution:**
- We don't know if the original population is normal
- Population could be skewed, bimodal, uniform, etc.
- **CLT says it doesn't matter!**

**2. Large Sample Size:**
- n = 100 ≥ 30 (well above threshold)
- Sample mean X̄ will be approximately normal regardless of population shape
- Larger n → better approximation

**3. Enables Inference:**
- Without CLT, we'd need to know population distribution
- CLT allows us to use normal distribution properties
- Makes statistical inference possible

---

#### **DETAILED CALCULATIONS**

**Alternative Approach - Confidence Level:**

This is equivalent to a **95.44% confidence interval:**

**Formula:** μ ± Z(σ/√n)

For 95.44% confidence, Z = 2.0:
```
60 ± 2.0(20/√100)
= 60 ± 2.0(2)
= 60 ± 4
= (56, 64)
```

**This matches our range exactly!**

---

#### **WHAT IF WE WANTED DIFFERENT PROBABILITIES?**

**Example 1: 90% confidence (want more samples to fall within range)**
```
For 90%: Z = 1.645
Margin = 1.645 × 2 = 3.29

P(|X̄ - 60| ≤ 3.29) = 0.90
Sample mean within ±3.29 of 60: 90% of the time
```

**Example 2: 99% confidence (want even higher probability)**
```
For 99%: Z = 2.576
Margin = 2.576 × 2 = 5.15

P(|X̄ - 60| ≤ 5.15) = 0.99
Sample mean within ±5.15 of 60: 99% of the time
```

---

#### **EFFECT OF SAMPLE SIZE**

**What if n = 25 instead of 100?**

```
SE = 20/√25 = 20/5 = 4

For ±4 margin:
Z = 4/4 = 1.0
P(-1.0 ≤ Z ≤ 1.0) = 0.6827 or 68.27%

Smaller sample → larger SE → lower probability for same margin
```

**What if n = 400?**
```
SE = 20/√400 = 20/20 = 1

For ±4 margin:
Z = 4/1 = 4.0
P(-4.0 ≤ Z ≤ 4.0) = 0.9999 or 99.99%

Larger sample → smaller SE → higher probability for same margin
```

**Key Insight:** Probability increases with sample size for fixed margin of error.

---

#### **VISUAL REPRESENTATION**

```

          __|__

   /        |        \

```

#### **REAL-WORLD IMPLICATIONS**

**FINAL ANSWER:**

## Question 5 (20 Marks)

### Part (i) - Epidemic Disease Treatment Effectiveness

**Question:** In an epidemic of certain diseases, 92 children contracted the disease. Of these 41 received no treatment and of these 10 showed after effects. Of the remainder who did receive the treatment, 17 showed after effects. Test hypothesis that the treatment was not effective.

---

**Given Data:**
- Total children: 92
- No treatment group: 41 (10 showed after effects)
- Treatment group: 51 (17 showed after effects)

---

**Step 1: Organize Data in Contingency Table**

| Group | After Effects | No After Effects | Total |
|-------|---------------|------------------|-------|
| **No Treatment** | 10 | 31 | 41 |
| **Treatment** | 17 | 34 | 51 |
| **Total** | 27 | 65 | 92 |

---

**Step 2: Calculate Proportions**

**No Treatment Group:**
```
After effects rate = 10/41 = 0.244 or 24.4%
```

**Treatment Group:**
```
After effects rate = 17/51 = 0.333 or 33.3%
```

**Observation:** Treatment group actually has **higher** after effects rate!

---

**Step 3: State Hypotheses**

**Null Hypothesis (H₀):** Treatment is not effective (no difference in after effects between groups)
```
H₀: p₁ = p₂
or
H₀: Treatment and After Effects are independent
```

**Alternative Hypothesis (H₁):** Treatment is effective (difference exists)
```
H₁: p₁ ≠ p₂
or
H₁: Treatment and After Effects are dependent
```

**Type:** Two-tailed test

---

**Step 4: Choose Test and Significance Level**

**Test:** Chi-Square (χ²) Test of Independence

**Formula:**
```
χ² = Σ[(Observed - Expected)² / Expected]
```

**Significance Level:** α = 0.05

---

**Step 5: Calculate Expected Frequencies**

**Formula for expected frequency:**
```
Expected = (Row Total × Column Total) / Grand Total
```

**Cell 1: No Treatment, After Effects**
```
E₁₁ = (41 × 27) / 92 = 1,107 / 92 = 12.03
```

**Cell 2: No Treatment, No After Effects**
```
E₁₂ = (41 × 65) / 92 = 2,665 / 92 = 28.97
```

**Cell 3: Treatment, After Effects**
```
E₂₁ = (51 × 27) / 92 = 1,377 / 92 = 14.97
```

**Cell 4: Treatment, No After Effects**
```
E₂₂ = (51 × 65) / 92 = 3,315 / 92 = 36.03
```

---

**Expected Frequencies Table:**

| Group | After Effects | No After Effects | Total |
|-------|---------------|------------------|-------|
| **No Treatment** | 12.03 | 28.97 | 41 |
| **Treatment** | 14.97 | 36.03 | 51 |
| **Total** | 27 | 65 | 92 |

---

**Step 6: Calculate Chi-Square Statistic**

**Formula:**
```
χ² = Σ[(O - E)² / E]
```

| Cell | Observed (O) | Expected (E) | (O - E) | (O - E)² | (O - E)²/E |
|------|-------------|-------------|---------|----------|------------|
| No Tx, After | 10 | 12.03 | -2.03 | 4.12 | 0.343 |
| No Tx, No After | 31 | 28.97 | 2.03 | 4.12 | 0.142 |
| Tx, After | 17 | 14.97 | 2.03 | 4.12 | 0.275 |
| Tx, No After | 34 | 36.03 | -2.03 | 4.12 | 0.114 |
| **Sum** | | | | | **0.874** |

```
χ² = 0.343 + 0.142 + 0.275 + 0.114
χ² = 0.874
```

**Test Statistic: χ² = 0.874**

---

**Step 7: Determine Degrees of Freedom**

**Formula:**
```
df = (rows - 1) × (columns - 1)
df = (2 - 1) × (2 - 1)
df = 1 × 1
df = 1
```

---

**Step 8: Find Critical Value**

From Chi-Square table with df = 1 and α = 0.05:
```
χ²_critical = 3.841
```

**Decision Rule:**
```
Reject H₀ if χ² > 3.841
Fail to reject H₀ if χ² ≤ 3.841
```

---

**Step 9: Make Decision**

```
χ² = 0.874 < χ²_critical = 3.841
```

**Decision:** **Fail to reject H₀**

---

**Step 10: Calculate P-value**

For χ² = 0.874 with df = 1:
```
P-value ≈ 0.35 (from χ² table)
```

```
P-value (0.35) > α (0.05)
```

**This confirms:** Fail to reject H₀

---

#### **CONCLUSION**

**Technical Conclusion:**
"At the 5% significance level, we fail to reject the null hypothesis."

**Practical Interpretation:**
"There is **insufficient evidence** to conclude that the treatment was effective. The observed difference in after effects rates between the treatment group (33.3%) and no-treatment group (24.4%) is **not statistically significant**. This difference could easily be due to random chance."

---

#### **DETAILED ANALYSIS**

**1. What the Data Shows:**
- No treatment: 24.4% after effects (10/41)
- Treatment: 33.3% after effects (17/51)
- **Treatment group actually fared worse!**

**2. Why Not Statistically Significant:**
- χ² = 0.874 is very small
- P-value = 0.35 means 35% chance of seeing this difference by random chance
- This is **not rare** enough to reject H₀

**3. Possible Explanations:**

**a) Treatment truly ineffective:**
- Treatment doesn't reduce after effects
- May even be harmful (higher rate in treatment group)

**b) Sample size limitations:**
- Total n = 92 may be too small
- Unequal group sizes (41 vs 51)
- Natural variation could mask small effects

**c) Confounding factors:**
- Treatment group may have had more severe cases initially
- Selection bias (sicker children more likely to receive treatment)
- Age, nutrition, disease stage not controlled

**d) Treatment timing:**
- Treatment may have started too late
- Insufficient dosage or duration

---

#### **ALTERNATIVE ANALYSIS: Z-TEST FOR TWO PROPORTIONS**

Let's verify using two-proportion Z-test:

**Step 1: Define proportions**
```
p₁ = 10/41 = 0.244 (no treatment)
p₂ = 17/51 = 0.333 (treatment)
```

**Step 2: Calculate pooled proportion**
```
p̂ = (10 + 17) / (41 + 51)
p̂ = 27 / 92
p̂ = 0.293
```

**Step 3: Calculate standard error**
```
SE = √[p̂(1-p̂)(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)]
SE = √[0.293(0.707)(1/41 + 1/51)]
SE = √[0.207(0.0244 + 0.0196)]
SE = √[0.207 × 0.044]
SE = √0.00911
SE = 0.0954
```

**Step 4: Calculate Z-statistic**
```
Z = (p₁ - p₂) / SE
Z = (0.244 - 0.333) / 0.0954
Z = -0.089 / 0.0954
Z = -0.933
```

**Step 5: Find P-value**
```
For two-tailed test:
P-value = 2 × P(Z < -0.933)
P-value = 2 × 0.1762
P-value = 0.352 ≈ 0.35
```

**This matches our χ² result!** ✓

Note: χ² = Z² for 2×2 tables
```
(-0.933)² = 0.870 ≈ 0.874 ✓
```

```

### Part (ii) - Two Samples from Same Normal Population

**Question:** Two samples of sizes nine and eight gave the sums of squares of deviations from their respective means equal to 160 and 91 respectively. Can they be regarded as drawn from the same normal population?

---

**Given:**
- Sample 1: n₁ = 9, SS₁ = 160
- Sample 2: n₂ = 8, SS₂ = 91
- Test if both from same normal population (same variance)

---

**Step 1: State Hypotheses**

**Null Hypothesis (H₀):** Both samples from same population (equal variances)
```
H₀: σ₁² = σ₂²
```

**Alternative Hypothesis (H₁):** Samples from different populations (unequal variances)
```
H₁: σ₁² ≠ σ₂²
```

**Type:** Two-tailed test

---

**Step 2: Choose Test**

**F-test for Equality of Variances**

**Formula:**
```
F = s₁² / s₂²  (where s₁² > s₂²)
```

**Significance Level:** α = 0.05

---

**Step 3: Calculate Sample Variances**

**Formula:** s² = SS / (n - 1)

**Sample 1:**
```
s₁² = 160 / (9 - 1)
s₁² = 160 / 8
s₁² = 20
```

**Sample 2:**
```
s₂² = 91 / (8 - 1)
s₂² = 91 / 7
s₂² = 13
```

---

**Step 4: Calculate F-statistic**

**Put larger variance in numerator:**
```
F = s₁² / s₂²
F = 20 / 13
F = 1.538
```

**Test Statistic: F = 1.538**

---

**Step 5: Determine Degrees of Freedom**

```
df₁ = n₁ - 1 = 9 - 1 = 8 (numerator)
df₂ = n₂ - 1 = 8 - 1 = 7 (denominator)
```

---

**Step 6: Find Critical Value**

**For two-tailed test at α = 0.05:**
Use α/2 = 0.025 for each tail

From F-table with df₁ = 8, df₂ = 7:
```
F_critical (0.025, 8, 7) ≈ 4.53
```

**Decision Rule:**
```
Reject H₀ if F > 4.53
Fail to reject H₀ if F ≤ 4.53
```

---

**Step 7: Make Decision**

```
F = 1.538 < F_critical = 4.53
```

**Decision:** **Fail to reject H₀**

---

**Step 8: Calculate P-value**

For F = 1.538 with df₁ = 8, df₂ = 7:
```
P-value ≈ 0.60 (two-tailed)
```

```
P-value (0.60) > α (0.05)
```

**Confirms:** Fail to reject H₀

---

#### **CONCLUSION**

**Technical Conclusion:**
"At the 5% significance level, we fail to reject the null hypothesis of equal variances."

**Practical Interpretation:**
"The two samples **can be regarded as drawn from the same normal population**. The difference in sample variances (s₁² = 20 vs s₂² = 13) is **not statistically significant** and can be attributed to random sampling variation."

---

#### **DETAILED ANALYSIS**

**1. Variance Ratio:**
```
Ratio = s₁² / s₂² = 20 / 13 = 1.54
```
- Sample 1 variance is 1.54 times Sample 2 variance
- This is a modest difference (not unusual for random sampling)

**2. Standard Deviations:**
```
s₁ = √20 = 4.47
s₂ = √13 = 3.61

Ratio = 4.47 / 3.61 = 1.24
```
- Standard deviations differ by only 24%

**3. Why Not Significant:**
- F = 1.538 is well below critical value (4.53)
- Would need F > 4.53 to reject H₀
- Our F value is only 34% of critical value
- P-value = 0.60 means 60% chance of seeing this difference by chance
- Very common outcome if populations truly equal

---

#### **ASSUMPTIONS CHECK**

**F-test requires:**
1. ✓ **Normal populations** (stated in problem)
2. ✓ **Random independent samples** (assumed)
3. ✓ **Independence between samples** (assumed)

**Important:** F-test is **sensitive to non-normality**. If populations not normal, results unreliable.

---

#### **CONFIDENCE INTERVAL FOR VARIANCE RATIO**

**95% CI for σ₁²/σ₂²:**

**Formula:**
```
(s₁²/s₂²) / F_upper < σ₁²/σ₂² < (s₁²/s₂²) × F_lower
```

Where:
- F_upper = F(0.025, 8, 7) = 4.53
- F_lower = 1/F(0.025, 7, 8) = 1/4.90 = 0.204

```
1.538 / 4.53 < σ₁²/σ₂² < 1.538 × 4.90
0.340 < σ₁²/σ₂² < 7.54
```

**Interpretation:** We're 95% confident true variance ratio is between 0.34 and 7.54.
- Interval includes 1.0 → consistent with H₀ (equal variances)
- Wide interval → imprecise estimate due to small samples

---

#### **PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS**

**1. Pooled Variance Estimate:**

Since variances can be considered equal, we can pool them:

**Formula:**
```
s_pooled² = (SS₁ + SS₂) / (n₁ + n₂ - 2)
s_pooled² = (160 + 91) / (9 + 8 - 2)
s_pooled² = 251 / 15
s_pooled² = 16.73

s_pooled = √16.73 = 4.09
```

**This pooled estimate is more precise than individual estimates.**

---

**2. Implications for t-tests:**

If comparing means of these two samples:
- Can use **pooled two-sample t-test** (assumes equal variances)
- More powerful than Welch's t-test
- Degrees of freedom: n₁ + n₂ - 2 = 15

---

**3. Sample Size Considerations:**

**Power of F-test with small samples:**
- n₁ = 9, n₂ = 8 are small
- F-test has **low power** for small samples
- Might miss real differences (Type II error)
- To detect variance ratio of 2.0 with 80% power:
  - Would need n ≈ 30 per group

---

#### **ALTERNATIVE TEST: LEVENE'S TEST**

**More robust to non-normality:**

If normality assumption doubtful, use Levene's test:
- Based on absolute deviations from median
- Less sensitive to outliers
- More appropriate for real-world data

**However, given problem states "normal population," F-test is appropriate.**

### Part (i) - One Sample t-Test

**Question:** Explain how to conduct a one sample t-test. Provide an example scenario and interpret the results of the test.

---

#### **WHAT IS A ONE SAMPLE T-TEST?**

**Definition:** Statistical test to determine whether a sample mean significantly differs from a known or hypothesized population mean.

**When to Use:**
- Small sample size (n < 30) typically
- Population standard deviation (σ) **unknown**
- Need to use sample standard deviation (s)
- Population (approximately) normally distributed

---

#### **THE 7 STEPS OF ONE SAMPLE T-TEST**

---

### **STEP 1: STATE HYPOTHESES**

**Null Hypothesis (H₀):** Sample mean equals hypothesized value
```
H₀: μ = μ₀
```

**Alternative Hypothesis (H₁):** Depends on research question

**Two-tailed:**
```
H₁: μ ≠ μ₀  (mean is different)
```

**Right-tailed:**
```
H₁: μ > μ₀  (mean is greater)
```

**Left-tailed:**
```
H₁: μ < μ₀  (mean is less)
```

---

### **STEP 2: CHOOSE SIGNIFICANCE LEVEL**

```
α = 0.05 (most common)
```

---

### **STEP 3: CHECK ASSUMPTIONS**

**1. Random Sample:**
- Observations randomly selected from population

**2. Independence:**
- Observations independent of each other

**3. Normality:**
- Population approximately normally distributed
- Less critical for larger samples (n ≥ 15-20)
- Check with:
  - Histogram
  - Q-Q plot
  - Shapiro-Wilk test

---

### **STEP 4: CALCULATE TEST STATISTIC**

**T-Statistic Formula:**
```
t = (X̄ - μ₀) / (s/√n)
```

Where:
- X̄ = sample mean
- μ₀ = hypothesized population mean
- s = sample standard deviation
- n = sample size
- s/√n = standard error (SE)

---

### **STEP 5: DETERMINE DEGREES OF FREEDOM**

```
df = n - 1
```

---

### **STEP 6: FIND CRITICAL VALUE OR P-VALUE**

**Method 1: Critical Value**
- Look up t_critical from t-table using df and α
- Compare t to t_critical

**Method 2: P-value**
- Calculate probability of observing t (or more extreme) if H₀ true
- Compare p-value to α

---

### **STEP 7: MAKE DECISION AND INTERPRET**

**If |t| > t_critical OR p-value < α:**
- Reject H₀
- Significant difference exists

**If |t| ≤ t_critical OR p-value ≥ α:**
- Fail to reject H₀
- No significant difference

---

### Part (ii) - Production Quality Hypothesis Test

**Question:** Experience has shown that 20 percent of a manufactured product is of top quality. On a particular day, the production of 400 articles, only 50 are of top quality. Show that either the production of the day chosen was not a representative sample or the hypothesis of 20 percent was wrong. Based on the day's production, find also the 95 percent confidence limits for the percentage of top-quality products.

---

**Given:**
- Historical claim: 20% top quality (p₀ = 0.20)
- Sample: n = 400 articles

**Step 1: State Hypotheses**

np₀ = 400 × 0.20 = 80 ≥ 10 ✓
n(1-p₀) = 400 × 0.80 = 320 ≥ 10 ✓
```

**All conditions met → Can use Z-test**

**Step 4: Calculate Test Statistic**

**Formula for proportion Z-test:**
```

Z = -3.75
```

**Test Statistic: Z = -3.75**

**Step 5: Find Critical Value**

For two-tailed test at α = 0.05:
```
Z_critical = ±1.96
```

**Decision Rule:**
```

Fail to reject H₀ if -1.96 ≤ Z ≤ 1.96
```

**Step 6: Make Decision**

```
Z = -3.75 < -1.96
```

**Decision:** **Reject H₀**

**Step 7: Calculate P-value**

```
P(Z < -3.75) = 0.0001 (from Z-table)

For two-tailed:
P-value = 2 × 0.0001 = 0.0002
```

```

## Question 7 (20 Marks)

### Part (i) - ANOVA vs t-Tests

**Question:** Explain how ANOVA differs from t-tests in hypothesis testing. Discuss why ANOVA is preferable when comparing the means of more than two groups.

---

#### **FUNDAMENTAL DIFFERENCE**

**t-Test:**
- Compares **2 groups** only
- Tests if two means are significantly different
- Example: Treatment vs Control

**ANOVA (Analysis of Variance):**
- Compares **3 or more groups** simultaneously
- Tests if at least one mean differs from others
- Example: Treatment A vs B vs C vs Control

---

#### **WHY NOT MULTIPLE t-TESTS?**

**Problem with Multiple t-Tests:**

**Scenario:** Compare 4 treatment groups

**Using t-tests:**
- Need to compare: A vs B, A vs C, A vs D, B vs C, B vs D, C vs D
- Total comparisons: 6 t-tests

**The Multiple Testing Problem:**

**For each test:**
```
P(Type I error) = α = 0.05 (5%)
```

**For all tests combined:**
```
P(at least one Type I error) = 1 - (1 - α)^k

Where k = number of comparisons

For 6 comparisons:
= 1 - (1 - 0.05)^6
= 1 - (0.95)^6
= 1 - 0.735
= 0.265 or 26.5%!
```

**Instead of 5% error rate, we now have 26.5% error rate!**

This is called **inflation of familywise error rate**.

---

**For different numbers of groups:**

| Groups | Comparisons | Error Rate | Inflation |
|--------|-------------|------------|-----------|
| 2 | 1 | 5.0% | 1.0× |
| 3 | 3 | 14.3% | 2.9× |
| 4 | 6 | 26.5% | 5.3× |
| 5 | 10 | 40.1% | 8.0× |
| 6 | 15 | 53.7% | 10.7× |

**ANOVA solves this by testing all groups simultaneously with controlled α!**

---

#### **HOW ANOVA WORKS**

**Key Concept:** ANOVA partitions total variance into components

**Total Variance = Between-Group Variance + Within-Group Variance**

**Between-Group Variance (Signal):**
- Variation due to different group means
- If groups truly different, this will be large

**Within-Group Variance (Noise):**
- Variation within each group
- Random error/individual differences
- Expected even if groups identical

**F-Statistic:**
```
F = (Between-Group Variance) / (Within-Group Variance)
F = (Signal) / (Noise)
```

**If F is large → groups differ significantly**

---

#### **HYPOTHESES**

**t-Test:**
```
H₀: μ₁ = μ₂
H₁: μ₁ ≠ μ₂
```

**ANOVA:**
```
H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃ = ... = μₖ (all means equal)
H₁: At least one mean differs
```

**Note:** H₁ doesn't specify which means differ—just that not all equal

---

#### **FORMULAS COMPARISON**

**t-Test (two independent samples):**
```
t = (X̄₁ - X̄₂) / √[s²(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)]

Where s² is pooled variance
```

**ANOVA F-Test:**
```
F = MSB / MSW

MSB = SSB / (k-1)  [Mean Square Between]
MSW = SSW / (N-k)  [Mean Square Within]

Where:
k = number of groups
N = total sample size
```

**Relationship:** For 2 groups, F = t²

---

#### **DETAILED ANOVA COMPONENTS**

**1. Total Sum of Squares (SST):**
```
SST = Σ(Xᵢⱼ - X̄_grand)²
```
Total variability in all data

**2. Between-Group Sum of Squares (SSB):**
```
SSB = Σnⱼ(X̄ⱼ - X̄_grand)²
```
Variability due to group differences

**3. Within-Group Sum of Squares (SSW):**
```
SSW = Σ Σ(Xᵢⱼ - X̄ⱼ)²
```
Variability within groups (error)

**Partition:**
```
SST = SSB + SSW
```

---

#### **ANOVA TABLE**

| Source | SS | df | MS | F |
|--------|----|----|----|----|
| **Between Groups** | SSB | k-1 | MSB = SSB/(k-1) | MSB/MSW |
| **Within Groups** | SSW | N-k | MSW = SSW/(N-k) | |
| **Total** | SST | N-1 | | |

---

#### **ASSUMPTIONS**

**t-Test Assumptions:**
1. Independent samples
2. Normal distributions
3. Equal variances (for pooled t-test)

**ANOVA Assumptions:**
1. Independent observations
2. Normal distributions (in each group)
3. **Homogeneity of variance** (equal variances across all groups)
4. Random samples

**Similar assumptions, but ANOVA extends to multiple groups**

---

#### **ADVANTAGES OF ANOVA**

**1. Controls Type I Error Rate:**
- Single test with α = 0.05
- No inflation from multiple comparisons
- **Most important advantage**

**2. More Powerful:**
- Uses all data simultaneously
- More efficient estimate of error variance
- Better at detecting true differences

**3. Comprehensive:**
- Tests overall hypothesis first
- Can follow up with post-hoc tests if significant
- Structured approach

**4. Flexibility:**
- Can handle unequal sample sizes
- Can extend to multiple factors (Two-way ANOVA, etc.)
- Basis for more complex designs

**5. Interpretation:**
- One overall test result
- Clear yes/no answer: "Do groups differ?"
- Then investigate which groups differ

---

#### **DISADVANTAGES OF ANOVA**

**1. Less Specific:**
- Doesn't tell which groups differ
- Need post-hoc tests for pairwise comparisons
- t-test directly compares two specific groups

**2. Assumptions:**
- Sensitive to variance heterogeneity
- Normality assumption across all groups
- Violations more complex to handle

**3. Interpretation with Many Groups:**
- Significant F doesn't identify where differences lie
- Need follow-up testing (increases complexity)

---

#### **POST-HOC TESTS (AFTER SIGNIFICANT ANOVA)**

If ANOVA is significant, use post-hoc tests to find which pairs differ:

**Common Post-Hoc Tests:**

**1. Tukey's HSD (Honestly Significant Difference):**
- Most popular
- Controls familywise error rate
- All pairwise comparisons

**2. Bonferroni:**
- Conservative (reduces α for each comparison)
- α_adjusted = α / number of comparisons

**3. Scheffé:**
- Most conservative
- For complex contrasts

**4. Dunnett:**
- Comparing all groups to one control group

**Example:**
```
ANOVA: F = 8.5, p < 0.001 → Reject H₀

Post-hoc (Tukey):
A vs B: p = 0.002 (significant)
A vs C: p = 0.156 (not significant)
B vs C: p = 0.025 (significant)

Conclusion: Groups A and B differ; B and C differ; A and C don't differ
```

---

#### **EXAMPLE: COMPARING TEACHING METHODS**

**Scenario:** Compare 4 teaching methods on exam scores

**Data:**
- Method A (Traditional): n=10, X̄=75, s=5
- Method B (Online): n=10, X̄=82, s=6
- Method C (Hybrid): n=10, X̄=80, s=5.5
- Method D (Flipped): n=10, X̄=78, s=5.8

---

**Approach 1: Multiple t-Tests (WRONG)**

**6 comparisons needed:**
```
A vs B, A vs C, A vs D, B vs C, B vs D, C vs D

Familywise error rate = 1 - (0.95)^6 = 26.5%
```

**Problem:** 26.5% chance of false positive!

---

**Approach 2: ANOVA (CORRECT)**

**Step 1: Calculate group means**
```
X̄_A = 75, X̄_B = 82, X̄_C = 80, X̄_D = 78
X̄_grand = (75+82+80+78)/4 = 78.75
```

**Step 2: Calculate SSB (Between)**
```
SSB = 10[(75-78.75)² + (82-78.75)² + (80-78.75)² + (78-78.75)²]
SSB = 10[14.06 + 10.56 + 1.56 + 0.56]
SSB = 10 × 26.74
SSB = 267.4
```

**Step 3: Calculate SSW (Within)**
```
For each group: SS = (n-1)s²

SS_A = 9 × 25 = 225
SS_B = 9 × 36 = 324
SS_C = 9 × 30.25 = 272.25
SS_D = 9 × 33.64 = 302.76

SSW = 225 + 324 + 272.25 + 302.76 = 1,124.01
```

**Step 4: Calculate Mean Squares**
```
MSB = SSB/(k-1) = 267.4/3 = 89.13
MSW = SSW/(N-k) = 1,124.01/36 = 31.22
```

**Step 5: Calculate F**
```
F = MSB/MSW = 89.13/31.22 = 2.85
```

**Step 6: Critical value**
```
F_critical(3, 36, 0.05) ≈ 2.87
```

**Decision:**
```
F = 2.85 < 2.87 → Marginally not significant (close!)
```

**With p-value ≈ 0.051, borderline significant**

---

**ANOVA Table:**

| Source | SS | df | MS | F | p-value |
|--------|----|----|----|----|---------|
| Between | 267.4 | 3 | 89.13 | 2.85 | 0.051 |
| Within | 1124.0 | 36 | 31.22 | | |
| Total | 1391.4 | 39 | | | |

---

**Interpretation:**
"At the 5% significance level, there is marginally insufficient evidence to conclude that the four teaching methods differ in effectiveness (F(3,36) = 2.85, p = .051). However, the result is borderline, suggesting possible differences that a larger sample might detect."

---

#### **WHEN TO USE WHICH TEST**

**Use t-Test when:**
- ✓ Comparing exactly **2 groups**
- ✓ Specific comparison pre-planned
- ✓ Simple, focused question
- ✓ Examples: Drug vs Placebo; Men vs Women

**Use ANOVA when:**
- ✓ Comparing **3 or more groups**
- ✓ Overall question: "Do groups differ?"
- ✓ Want to control familywise error rate
- ✓ Examples: Multiple treatments; Several age groups

---

#### **SPECIAL CASE: 2 GROUPS**

**For 2 groups, ANOVA and t-test are equivalent:**

```
F = t²

If t-test gives t = 2.5:
ANOVA will give F = (2.5)² = 6.25

Both have same p-value
Both lead to same conclusion
```

**Generally, use t-test for 2 groups (simpler interpretation)**

---

#### **EFFECT SIZE**

**t-Test:**
```
Cohen's d = (X̄₁ - X̄₂) / s_pooled
```

**ANOVA:**
```
Eta-squared (η²) = SSB / SST
Omega-squared (ω²) = (SSB - (k-1)MSW) / (SST + MSW)
```

**Interpretation:**
- η² = 0.01: small effect
- η² = 0.06: medium effect
- η² = 0.14: large effect

---

#### **EXTENSIONS**

**t-Test:**
- Paired t-test (related samples)
- Welch's t-test (unequal variances)

**ANOVA:**
- **One-Way ANOVA** (one factor, multiple groups)
- **Two-Way ANOVA** (two factors)
- **Repeated Measures ANOVA** (within-subjects)
- **MANOVA** (multiple dependent variables)
- **ANCOVA** (with covariates)

**ANOVA is gateway to more sophisticated analyses**

---

#### **PRACTICAL EXAMPLE: DRUG DOSAGES**

**Research Question:** Which dosage of new drug is most effective?

**Groups:**
- Placebo (0mg)
- Low dose (50mg)
- Medium dose (100mg)
- High dose (150mg)

**Why ANOVA preferred:**

**1. Single Test:**
- One F-test answers "Do dosages differ?"
- α = 0.05 maintained

**2. Efficiency:**
- All data used to estimate error variance
- More precise than separate t-tests

**3. Follow-up:**
- If significant, use post-hoc tests:
  - Which dose beats placebo?
  - Is more better (dose-response)?
  - Optimal dosage?

**4. Interpretation:**
- First: "Overall, do dosages matter?" (ANOVA)
- Then: "Which specific dosages differ?" (Post-hoc)

---

**SUMMARY:**

| Aspect | t-Test | ANOVA |
|--------|--------|-------|
| **Groups** | 2 | 3+ |
| **Null Hypothesis** | μ₁ = μ₂ | All μ equal |
| **Test Statistic** | t | F |
| **Type I Error** | α (2 groups only) | α (multiple groups) |
| **Multiple Comparisons** | Problem with >2 | Handles naturally |
| **Specificity** | Direct comparison | Need post-hoc |
| **Power** | Good for 2 groups | Better for 3+ groups |
| **Use When** | 2 groups | 3+ groups |

**Key Principle:** ANOVA is preferable for 3+ groups because it controls familywise error rate while simultaneously testing all groups, providing both efficiency and proper error control that multiple t-tests cannot achieve.
